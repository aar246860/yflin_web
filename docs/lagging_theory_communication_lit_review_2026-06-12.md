# Lagging Theory Communication Literature Review

Date: 2026-06-12

Purpose: redesign the public website around Lagging Theory first, using science communication, persuasion, and B2B thought-leadership principles. This is a strategy note, not public copy.

## Practical Interpretation

The site should not behave like a lab recruitment page or a complete CV. It should create a clear memory link between Ying-Fan Lin and a specific class of problems:

> delayed groundwater or thermal responses whose interpretation changes engineering decisions.

That link is stronger than a broad claim such as "groundwater uncertainty" because it gives future collaborators a concrete situation in which to remember the work.

## Evidence From Communication Research

### 1. Science communication starts with audience, goal, and context

The National Academies report on science communication emphasizes that communication strategy must be aligned with goals, audiences, and context. For this website, the goal is not to explain every paper. The goal is to help high-value teams recognize a delayed-response problem and see Lin as a credible person to contact.

Implication:
- Write for expert collaborators and decision-making teams, not students.
- Use a clear problem trigger before technical detail.
- Make uncertainty explicit without sounding defensive.

Source: National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine, *Communicating Science Effectively: A Research Agenda* (2017), https://www.nationalacademies.org/read/23674

### 2. Expert audiences require central-route persuasion

The Elaboration Likelihood Model distinguishes between persuasion through argument quality and persuasion through surface cues. Technical collaborators, reviewers, and industrial partners have high motivation to evaluate claims, so the site must prioritize argument quality: mechanism, evidence, limits, and decision relevance.

Implication:
- The homepage can be memorable, but proof must appear quickly.
- Avoid overclaiming that lagging theory replaces Darcy, Theis, Neuman, dual-porosity, or delayed-yield models.
- Show the evidence path: field signal -> model pathway -> validation -> decision endpoint.

Source: Petty and Cacioppo, "The Elaboration Likelihood Model of Persuasion" (1986), https://richardepetty.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/01/1986-advances-pettycacioppo.pdf

### 3. Marketing should build memory before the buyer is active

The B2B Institute's 95-5 rule argues that many future buyers are not currently in-market, so communication should build memory links to relevant future buying situations. For an academic site, "buyer" means a future collaborator, PI, agency, company, or conference organizer.

Implication:
- Create category entry points, not only publication lists.
- Make repeated problem phrases: delayed drawdown, delayed recovery, phase lag, TRT mismatch, residual structure, decision endpoint.
- The site should make people think "Lin" when a delayed groundwater or thermal response appears.

Source: LinkedIn B2B Institute, "95-5 Rule", https://business.linkedin.com/advertise/resources/b2b-institute/b2b-research/trends/95-5-rule

### 4. Category entry points make expertise retrievable

Category entry points are the situations, needs, or triggers that cause someone to recall a brand. For Lin's site, the useful category is not "groundwater" in general. It is a narrower category:

> delayed-response interpretation for groundwater and subsurface energy decisions.

Implication:
- Do not dilute the homepage with every research theme equally.
- Use Lagging Theory as the first concept, then make Groundwater Memory, Transformation Uncertainty, and Subsurface Energy Intelligence follow from it.
- Use field notes as repeated entry points, each tied to one problem situation.

Source: LinkedIn B2B Institute, "Category Entry Points in a B2B World", https://business.linkedin.com/advertise/resources/b2b-institute/cep-in-b2b

### 5. Thought leadership is a trust-building asset

Edelman and LinkedIn describe B2B thought leadership as a tool for building trust, alignment, and access to decision-makers. The relevant lesson is not to sound like a salesperson. It is to show original thinking that helps a high-value audience understand a difficult problem better.

Implication:
- Publish short field notes that expose a useful way of thinking.
- Use a restrained tone: specific, testable, and decision-focused.
- Make the collaboration page ask for a problem brief, not a vague meeting.

Source: Edelman and LinkedIn, *2025 B2B Thought Leadership Impact Report*, https://www.edelman.com/expertise/Business-Marketing/2025-b2b-thought-leadership-report

### 6. Positioning needs alternatives, difference, and proof

April Dunford's positioning framework is useful here because Lagging Theory must be positioned against alternatives without attacking them. The site should define who it is for, what alternatives exist, why this frame is different, and what proof supports it.

Implication:
- Alternative approaches: classical aquifer-test models, delayed yield, leaky aquifer, dual-porosity, numerical inverse models, empirical curve fitting.
- Difference: lagging theory is presented as a disciplined delayed-response diagnostic and transformation pathway.
- Proof: 2017 WRR origin, later WRR/Journal of Hydrology/AWR extensions, TRT and heat-transport applications, and internal TU_Lag known-truth tests.

Source: April Dunford, "A Quickstart Guide to Positioning", https://www.aprildunford.com/post/a-quickstart-guide-to-positioning

## Communication Rule For This Website

Use this hierarchy:

1. Problem trigger: the response arrives late.
2. Insight: delay can be information, not only noise.
3. Discipline: the model must pass residual, complexity, identifiability, validation, and decision gates.
4. Evidence: published lineage from 2017 onward.
5. Application: pumping tests, groundwater time series, TRT, shallow geothermal, and decision propagation.
6. Action: send a delayed-response problem brief.

## Main Risk

The public claim will fail if it says:

> Lagging Theory is the correct physical model for all delayed groundwater response.

The stronger and safer claim is:

> Lagging Theory provides a testable pathway for diagnosing flux-gradient, free-surface, and phase-amplitude delays when standard groundwater interpretations leave decision-relevant residual structure.

# Lagging Theory Scientific Correction Note

Date: 2026-06-12

Purpose: record the scientific tightening applied after re-checking the core Lagging Theory literature.

## Correction

The first public draft framed Lagging Theory as a broad "delayed groundwater and thermal response" language. That is useful for positioning, but it is not precise enough for expert readers.

The corrected framing is:

> Lagging Theory begins as a generalized Darcy-law framework that allows water flux and hydraulic gradient or drawdown gradient to occur at different times. Later work extends the same delayed-response logic to free-surface drainage in unconfined aquifers, response functions for groundwater time series, and periodic head signals where phase and amplitude imply different apparent diffusivities.

## Core Technical Points

1. Lin and Yeh (2017) introduced phase lags in water flux and drawdown gradient for leaky confined aquifer pumping.
2. The flux lag is associated with fast transient adjustment, including inertial effects in high-permeability paths.
3. The gradient or head-development lag is associated with structural interaction, including noninterconnected pores or fractures.
4. If the two lag times are equal in the 2017 formulation, the lagging effect disappears.
5. Lin et al. (2019) applied the lagging idea to unconfined aquifer pumping by deriving a free-surface equation with lag times related to capillary-fringe release and capillary-suction delayed drainage.
6. Lin and Kurylyk (2026) used flux lag and head lag to separate amplitude damping from phase delay in periodic groundwater signals.

## Website Rule

Use phrases such as:

- generalized Darcy-law Lagging Theory;
- flux-gradient lag;
- free-surface drainage lag;
- phase-amplitude mismatch;
- field-scale causal memory;
- constitutive or boundary-response diagnostic.

Avoid phrases that make the concept sound like:

- a universal replacement for Darcy, Theis, Neuman, delayed yield, leakage, or dual-porosity models;
- a generic metaphor for all groundwater memory;
- a fully established thermal theory rather than a groundwater-aware extension toward thermal-response interpretation.

## One-Sentence Thesis

林穎凡的研究不是從「提出一個 lag model」開始，而是從地下水解析解傳統出發，逐步把邊界、井周、非線性、雙孔隙、非平衡、熱傳與場址尺度記憶等複雜機制納入可檢查的模型框架，最後轉向一個更務實的問題：當真實地下系統無法被完全還原時，工程決策如何稽核模型轉換本身帶來的不確定性。

Short English version:

Ying-Fan Lin's work begins with analytical groundwater theory, expands through boundary physics, nonlinear exchange, lagging response, heat transport, and subsurface-energy interpretation, and now converges on a decision-oriented question: how much uncertainty is introduced when field responses are transformed into apparent parameters and engineering limits.

## 中文正式版：研究脈絡反思

回看 2016 到 2026 年的著作脈絡，我的研究並不是單純從某一個模型、某一個理論或某一個應用題目出發。比較準確的說法是：我長期關心的是地下水與地下熱傳系統中，觀測反應如何被轉換成可解釋的物理量、可估計的參數，以及最後可用於工程判斷的決策邊界。

早期的研究以解析解與半解析解為核心。這些工作處理抽水試驗、含水層邊界、井周效應、非飽和流、河川與含水層交互作用、污染物傳輸與熱傳問題。表面上看，這些研究像是在不同場景中推導新的解；但它們真正累積的是一種研究能力：在複雜現地系統被壓縮成可分析的問題時，仍然保留足夠的物理結構，讓研究者可以判斷某一個反應究竟來自邊界、井周、儲水釋放、交換作用，還是只是模型配適的自由度。

這使得解析解在我的研究中不是簡化現實的藉口，而是一種基準工具。它提供可檢查的參考行為，讓後續更複雜的機制有比較對象。沒有這種基準，就很難判斷現地資料中的異常反應是真實機制、資料雜訊、邊界影響，還是錯誤模型造成的表觀參數。

第二條脈絡是 Lagging Theory。這條線不應該被描述成一般意義上的 delay model。比較嚴格的理解是：Lagging Theory 是用來檢查地下水通量、 hydraulic gradient、水位變化、自由水面、邊界反應或週期性水頭訊號是否存在非同步調整。這種非同步不一定只有一種成因；它可能來自滲漏、儲水交換、非飽和排水、裂隙與基質交換、非連通孔隙、邊界儲水、異質路徑或水力與力學耦合。

因此，Lagging Theory 的意義不只是增加一個可配適參數，而是提供一種診斷語言：當傳統模型可以擬合資料，但無法同時解釋振幅、相位、恢復過程、邊界反應或工程端點時，lagging response 是否代表一種被忽略的地下水記憶或非平衡機制？這也是為什麼後續研究會自然延伸到地下水時間序列、週期性水頭訊號、地層下陷、邊界辨識與抽水反應的再詮釋。

第三條脈絡是複雜機制的可解釋化。從雙孔隙、非線性交換、非平衡溶質傳輸、井周破壞、井損、河川枯竭、海岸含水層、斷層帶到有限資訊下的反演問題，這些題目看似分散，但共同點是：傳統水文參數常常只是某個模型路徑下的表觀結果。若不同機制都能解釋同一組觀測資料，研究重點就不能只停留在哪一個模型 fit 最好，而必須問哪一個機制在資料中具有可辨識性，以及它是否真的改變預測與決策。

第四條脈絡是地下熱傳與地下能源。從 aquifer heat transport、temperature-depth profile、vertical groundwater flux、borehole thermal energy storage、thermal response test 到 shallow geothermal application，這些工作不是離開地下水領域，而是把同一個解釋問題移到熱與能源場景。熱響應試驗與淺層地熱設計同樣面臨觀測反應到參數的轉換：現地熱訊號會受到地下水流動、熱擴散、邊界、灌漿材料、場址尺度與異質性影響。若這些因素被錯誤處理，得到的熱傳參數與設計安全邊界也會偏移。

最後一條脈絡是不確定性的妥協。這裡的「妥協」不是降低科學要求，而是承認現地地下水問題很少能完全識別真實機制、真實參數與真實幾何。更複雜的模型可能增加擬合能力，卻降低可辨識性；更簡單的模型可能方便工程使用，卻把偏差藏在表觀參數中。因此，真正重要的問題不是某一個模型是否永遠正確，而是不同模型路徑如何把觀測反應轉換成不同的參數、不同的不確定性，以及不同的工程判斷。

這就是 transformation uncertainty 的位置。它把不確定性分析往前推，不只分析地下水模型輸入參數的不確定性，也稽核「觀測反應如何變成參數」這一步。抽水試驗、恢復資料、水位時間序列或熱響應資料本身是觀測；transmissivity、storage、thermal conductivity、response time、allowable pumping 或 risk boundary 則是經由模型詮釋得到的結果。若模型路徑不同，決策端點也可能不同。

因此，我的研究主軸可以被總結為：從解析解建立可檢查的基準，從複雜機制中辨識可解釋的反應結構，從 Lagging Theory 捕捉非同步與地下水記憶，最後用 transformation uncertainty 評估這些模型詮釋如何影響工程決策。這條路線的目標不是宣稱某個模型取代所有模型，而是建立一套更嚴格的判斷方式：只有當額外機制能改善殘差結構、提高預測能力、維持參數可辨識性，並且改變工程端點時，它才值得被納入。

## Phase 1. Analytical Solutions as Controlled Baselines, 2016-2020

The first phase is best understood as training in controlled reference behavior. The key question was not simply how to derive another closed-form solution. The deeper question was how to make field complexity mathematically visible without losing the ability to check the result.

Representative works include:

- 2016, finite two-zone confined aquifers with Robin-type outer boundary.
- 2017, constant-head pumping in leaky unconfined aquifers with unsaturated-flow effects.
- 2019, heat transfer with conduction and dispersion in aquifers under Robin-type injection-well boundaries.
- 2020, stream-aquifer analytical models with Robin boundary conditions representing streambed width and storage.
- 2020, contaminant diffusion through geomembrane and unsaturated soil liner with sorption and Robin-type boundary.
- 2020, constant-head pumping with a rate-dependent factor at the well screen.

The intellectual function of this phase was to establish a modeler's discipline: before arguing that a field response is anomalous, one needs a defensible baseline. Analytical and semi-analytical solutions provide that baseline. They let one separate what is caused by pumping geometry, boundary leakage, wellbore condition, unsaturated release, or transport coupling from what is merely fitting flexibility.

This phase also explains why later work did not abandon analytical thinking when moving into more complex topics. The analytical solution is not a nostalgic tool. It is a way to ask whether a mechanism is identifiable, whether a parameter has a stable interpretation, and whether a model is simple enough to be tested against alternatives.

## Phase 2. Lagging Theory as Flux-Gradient Asynchrony, 2017-2022

The second phase introduced the line that later became the core research identity: Lagging Theory. The crucial point is that this line should not be framed as a simple delay model. A simple delay shifts a signal in time. Lagging Theory asks a more physical and more diagnostic question: when do groundwater flux, hydraulic gradient, drawdown, head development, free-surface motion, or boundary response move out of step enough to change interpretation?

Representative works include:

- 2017, lagging model for drawdown induced by constant-rate pumping in a leaky confined aquifer.
- 2019, unconfined flow induced by constant-rate pumping based on Lagging Theory.
- 2022, wedge-shaped aquifer revisited by Lagging Theory.
- 2024, groundwater time series with limited pumping information using a response function based on Lagging Theory.
- 2025, lagging Darcy flow and land subsidence in a three-layer groundwater pumping system.
- 2026, Lagging Theory for periodic hydraulic head signals in aquifers.

The conceptual shift is from "find a better aquifer-test curve" to "diagnose asynchronous hydraulic response." In this sense, lag is not a single causal explanation. It is an observable trace that may arise from leakage, storage exchange, unsaturated drainage, fracture-matrix interaction, noninterconnected pores, hydro-mechanical coupling, inertia, boundary storage, tortuous pathways, or unresolved heterogeneity.

This is why the later formulation can connect to dual-porosity models, delayed-yield models, hydraulic creep, and periodic signal propagation. Mathematical similarity does not necessarily weaken the theory. It can show that different physical mechanisms share a common non-instantaneous response structure. The scientific risk is overclaiming universality; the scientific opportunity is to develop a disciplined diagnostic framework for when asynchronous response matters.

## Phase 3. Complex Mechanisms Without Losing Interpretability, 2021-2025

The third phase broadened the mechanism space. The research no longer focused only on canonical pumping problems, but also on nonlinear exchange, dual-porosity flow, near-well damage, wellbore skin, stream depletion, coastal aquifers, fault zones, and limited-information inverse problems.

Representative works include:

- 2021, dual-porosity flow with a generalized nonlinear water-transfer term.
- 2022, slug-test interpretation considering near-well formation damage and nonlinear flow.
- 2023, physically or chemically non-equilibrium solute transport in heterogeneous porous media.
- 2023, hydrogeological parameter estimation at observation wells without pumping-well information.
- 2023, spatiotemporal groundwater pumping-process estimation using data-driven approaches.
- 2024, semi-analytical stream drawdown and stream depletion under pumping.
- 2025, high-permeability wellbore skin effect on aquifer response.
- 2025, fast identification of hydrogeological boundaries and aquifer parameters.
- 2025, tidal-signal propagation in coastal aquifers with semi-permeable boundaries and partial penetration.
- 2025, groundwater modeling through anisotropic fault zones.
- 2025, island aquifers with pumping, recharge, and complex coastlines.

This phase is best interpreted as a sustained effort to keep complex mechanisms interpretable. A purely numerical approach can include many processes, but it may hide which process controls the observation. A purely classical analytical approach remains transparent, but it can misrepresent field complexity. Lin's work increasingly occupies the middle ground: use analytical or semi-analytical structures to expose where the classical interpretation fails and what mechanism is responsible.

This middle ground is important because it creates a bridge to uncertainty. If a fitted transmissivity or storage coefficient is only the output of a particular interpretation pathway, then the parameter is not a direct property of the aquifer. It is a model-conditioned apparent parameter.

## Phase 4. From Groundwater Flow to Heat and Subsurface Energy, 2019-2026

The fourth phase transfers the same logic into heat transport, thermal response tests, and shallow geothermal or subsurface-energy interpretation. This should not be described as leaving groundwater for thermal engineering. It is better described as moving the same interpretive problem into a more decision-intensive setting.

Representative works include:

- 2019, heat transfer with conduction and dispersion in aquifers with injection-well Robin boundary.
- 2022, transient vertical groundwater flux from temperature-depth profiles.
- 2022, rainfall infiltration rates from heat-transfer models and subsurface temperature profiles.
- 2023, soil type and thermal-boundary effects on predicted temperature profiles and groundwater fluxes.
- 2024, image-well method for transient borehole thermal energy storage with complex boundaries.
- 2026, grout heat storage effects in thermal response tests.
- 2026, non-integer dimension modeling and sensitivity diagnostics for thermal response tests.
- 2026, topology-driven bridging and matrix-dominated blocking in random thermal interfaces.

Thermal response tests and shallow geothermal design have the same structural problem as aquifer tests: a field signal is transformed into apparent parameters through a model pathway. If groundwater flow, grout heat storage, thermal dispersion, field support, boundary condition, or non-integer transport dimension is misrepresented, the apparent thermal conductivity or design margin may be biased.

The strategic value of this phase is that it makes the research more application-facing. Pumping-test interpretation is scientifically important, but subsurface energy, industrial heat, and water-energy planning give the same theoretical questions a more visible engineering surface.

## Phase 5. Transformation Uncertainty as a Necessary Compromise, 2024-2026+

The fifth phase is the most mature form of the research program. It accepts a difficult truth: field hydrogeology cannot always identify the true mechanism, true parameter, or true geometry. More complex models may improve fit while weakening identifiability. Simpler models may be operationally useful while introducing hidden bias.

Transformation uncertainty is the proposed compromise. Instead of asking only which model fits best, it asks:

- What is the measured object?
- Through which model pathway is it transformed?
- What apparent parameter is produced?
- How much bias or dispersion does this transformation introduce?
- Does the difference propagate to an engineering endpoint such as allowable pumping, recovery time, thermal design, dewatering margin, or risk boundary?

Representative connecting works include:

- 2024, groundwater time series with limited pumping information.
- 2025, Rethinking Aquifer Characterization: Insights from Lagging Models.
- 2025-2026, lagging flow, subsidence, periodic head signals, and TRT-related studies.
- The ongoing TU_Lag line, which treats drawdown-to-parameter interpretation as a front-end uncertainty source before downstream groundwater modeling or engineering decisions.

This reframes the research program. The goal is no longer to prove that one model is universally superior. The goal is to audit when a model pathway changes the decision. That is a stronger and more defensible scientific position.

## Representative Problem Evolution

The evolution can be summarized as a sequence of increasingly difficult questions:

1. What does a controlled aquifer system do under an idealized boundary?
2. What happens when the boundary, well screen, unsaturated zone, or streambed has its own physics?
3. What happens when flux, gradient, head, or boundary response are not synchronous?
4. What happens when the same non-instantaneous structure appears as dual porosity, delayed drainage, leakage, solute non-equilibrium, or thermal response?
5. What happens when several plausible mechanisms can fit the same observation?
6. How much engineering decision error is introduced by choosing one interpretation pathway over another?

This is the path from analytical solution to mechanism diagnosis to uncertainty audit.

## Academic Reflection

The strength of this research program is not that every paper belongs to one named theory. It is that the papers repeatedly return to one epistemic problem: hydrogeologists do not observe aquifer properties directly. They observe responses. Parameters, mechanisms, and safe operating limits are inferred through models.

The early analytical papers established how to construct defensible response models. The Lagging Theory papers exposed a class of asynchronous responses that classical Darcy-flow interpretations may hide. The dual-porosity, nonlinear, boundary, and thermal papers showed that many field processes can produce similar non-instantaneous signatures. The transformation-uncertainty line then accepts the consequence: if different plausible pathways transform the same measured response into different parameters or decisions, then the transformation itself must be audited.

This is also the most important guardrail against overclaiming. Lagging Theory should not be promoted as a universal replacement for Darcy's law, Theis, Neuman, delayed-yield, dual-porosity, leakage, or numerical groundwater models. Its defensible role is narrower and stronger: it is a testable diagnostic pathway for flux-gradient asynchrony and groundwater memory. It earns its place only when it improves residual structure, held-out prediction, parameter transfer, or engineering endpoints after accounting for model complexity and identifiability.

The broader contribution is therefore not just a model. It is a research style: build analytical baselines, expose hidden mechanisms, test whether added mechanism changes decisions, and quantify the uncertainty introduced when field responses are converted into design parameters.

## Website Narrative, English Version

Ying-Fan Lin's research develops interpretable models for groundwater and subsurface-energy systems where field responses cannot be treated as instantaneous, local, or uniquely interpretable.

The work began with analytical and semi-analytical solutions for aquifer tests, Robin-type boundaries, wellbore effects, stream-aquifer interaction, contaminant diffusion, and heat transport. These solutions provide controlled baselines for asking a recurring question: when does a field response depart from classical interpretation because a boundary, well, storage domain, or transport pathway has its own dynamics?

That question led to Lagging Theory, a framework for testing flux-gradient asynchrony in groundwater flow. In this view, lag is not a simple signal delay. It is a diagnostic trace of non-instantaneous hydraulic adjustment, which may arise from leakage, delayed drainage, fracture-matrix exchange, noninterconnected pore domains, hydro-mechanical coupling, boundary storage, or unresolved heterogeneity.

Later work extended this logic to groundwater memory, periodic head signals, dual-porosity and nonlinear exchange, fault-zone interpretation, coastal aquifers, stream depletion, land subsidence, thermal response tests, and shallow geothermal applications. Across these settings, the common problem is not merely calibration fit. It is whether the interpretation pathway changes the apparent parameter and the engineering decision.

The current research direction is transformation uncertainty: auditing the pathway from measured response to apparent parameter to decision endpoint. The aim is to support groundwater and subsurface-energy decisions that are not only model-based, but model-aware.

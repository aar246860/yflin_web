Operating thesis

Lag is a diagnostic trace, not a single cause.

In Lagging Theory, lag represents a measurable asynchrony among water flux, hydraulic gradient, head development, and boundary or free-surface response. It is not merely a signal-processing time shift; it can diagnose inertia, tortuous pathways, hydro-mechanical interaction, noninterconnected pores or fractures, capillary drainage, inter-domain exchange, and field-scale pathway equilibration.

What is Lagging Theory?

Lagging Theory is a generalized groundwater-flow framework for testing whether water flux, hydraulic gradient, drawdown, boundary response, or thermal response evolve out of phase. The lag is a measurable trace of asynchronous hydraulic adjustment, not a single assumed mechanism.

What is flux-gradient asynchrony?

Flux-gradient asynchrony means groundwater flux does not respond instantaneously or proportionally to the hydraulic gradient assumed by a classical Darcy-flow model. It can arise from tortuous pathways, inter-domain exchange, capillary drainage, leakage, inertia, heterogeneity, or hydro-mechanical coupling.

How is this different from simple delay?

A simple signal delay shifts a response in time. Lagging Theory asks whether different hydraulic quantities move out of phase in ways that change amplitude, recovery, parameter interpretation, or decision endpoints. The mechanism must be tested against residuals, identifiability, and held-out prediction.

When is it useful?

The framework is useful when classical well-test, recovery, periodic-head, thermal-response, or subsidence interpretations leave systematic timing or amplitude errors. It becomes engineering-relevant only when the alternative interpretation changes transmissivity, storage, pumping limits, recovery time, thermal design, or uncertainty buffers.

How does it connect to transformation uncertainty?

Transformation uncertainty asks how much decision error is introduced when measured responses are converted into parameters by a chosen model. Lagging Theory supplies one candidate transformation pathway for asynchronous systems, so its value can be tested by comparing decisions with and without the lagging interpretation.

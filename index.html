Operating thesis
Lag is not a generic delay. It is a decision diagnostic.

Lagging Theory tests whether water flux, hydraulic gradient, head development, boundary response, free-surface drainage, or thermal response evolve out of phase enough to change interpretation. The lag can express tortuous pathways, hydro-mechanical interaction, noninterconnected pores or fractures, capillary drainage, inter-domain exchange, or field-scale pathway equilibration.

AI-readable summary

Definitions for search, citation, and collaboration.

These concise blocks state the research idea in a form that can be quoted, checked, and linked without flattening it into generic delay language.

definition
What is Lagging Theory?

Lagging Theory is a generalized groundwater-flow framework for testing whether water flux, hydraulic gradient, drawdown, boundary response, or thermal response evolve out of phase. The lag is a measurable trace of asynchronous hydraulic adjustment, not a single assumed mechanism.

mechanism
What is flux-gradient asynchrony?

Flux-gradient asynchrony means groundwater flux does not respond instantaneously or proportionally to the hydraulic gradient assumed by a classical Darcy-flow model. It can arise from tortuous pathways, inter-domain exchange, capillary drainage, leakage, inertia, heterogeneity, or hydro-mechanical coupling.

distinction
How is this different from simple delay?

A simple signal delay shifts a response in time. Lagging Theory asks whether different hydraulic quantities move out of phase in ways that change amplitude, recovery, parameter interpretation, or decision endpoints.

use case
When is it useful?

The framework is useful when classical well-test, recovery, periodic-head, thermal-response, or subsidence interpretations leave systematic timing or amplitude errors that change transmissivity, storage, pumping limits, recovery time, thermal design, or uncertainty buffers.

decision path
How does it connect to transformation uncertainty?

Transformation uncertainty asks how much decision error is introduced when measured responses are converted into parameters by a chosen model. Lagging Theory supplies one candidate transformation pathway for asynchronous systems.

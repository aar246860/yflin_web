Lagging Theory for aquifer-test interpretation

Groundwater flux and hydraulic gradients do not always move in step.

Ying-Fan Lin studies what happens when aquifer-test records, recovery data, deformation signals, or thermal response tests carry timing that a classical instantaneous interpretation cannot absorb. The work stays close to analytical well hydraulics, then asks a decision question: does the mismatch change the parameter, the uncertainty range, or the engineering limit?

When does a pumping test need Lagging Darcy Law?

This interaction uses a constant-rate pumping setting to test the Lin and Yeh (2017) idea: water flux and drawdown gradient may respond at different macroscopic times. When those times separate, the early drawdown curve can carry information that a classical interpretation smooths away.

Calculation note: this teaching model applies the Lin-Yeh lag operator to a line-source leaky confined aquifer transfer function in Laplace space. The browser inverts the response with a 12-term Gaver-Stehfest algorithm and a Bessel K0 approximation. Wellbore storage and finite well radius are not included, so calibrated field analysis should replace this public demo before design use.

Diagnostic rule: if the flux lag and gradient lag are equal, the lagging term cancels and the curve returns to the classical response.

Research note

The work starts from a conservative premise.

If a traditional aquifer-test model is enough, use it. If timing mismatch survives the checks, treat it as physical information before it becomes hidden uncertainty.

What is measured

Drawdown is the observation.

The field record is the starting point. Transmissivity, storage, leakage, skin, and lag time are interpretations imposed through an analytical model.

What is tested

The timing of response is not assumed away.

Lagging Theory checks whether flux and gradient move together closely enough for a classical interpretation to carry the decision.

What changes

The endpoint is a design variable.

The result matters only when it moves a pumping limit, recovery time, thermal margin, or uncertainty buffer.

01

What is Lagging Theory?

A groundwater-flow interpretation that tests whether flux, gradient, drawdown, boundary response, or thermal response evolve out of phase at the aquifer-test scale.

02

What is not being claimed?

Lag is not treated as a universal replacement for Theis, Neuman, leakage, dual-porosity, or numerical models. It is used when the record carries timing and amplitude mismatch that changes interpretation.

03

Why connect it to uncertainty?

The same measured curve can lead to different parameters under different analytical models. Transformation uncertainty asks how far that model choice propagates into a decision.

                                           hydraulic parameters (Charbeneau, 2000). In the test, the aquifer is pumped with a constant discharge rate,
                                           and the temporal drawdown data are collected at observation wells. The hydraulic parameters are then
                                           determined by analyzing the measured data with analytical approaches coupled with an optimization
                                           method or a graphical type curve method. The behavior of unconﬁned ﬂow differs signiﬁcantly from that
                                           of conﬁned ﬂow if pumping continues long enough. The temporal distribution of measured drawdown data
                                           often demonstrates an S‐shaped curve, which may be divided into three segments as early time (ﬁrst seg-
                                           ment), intermediate time (second segment), and late time periods (third segment; Neuman, 1972). The
                                           pumped water is mainly from the aquifer storage in the ﬁrst segment. Later, the effect of the gravity drainage
                                           due to the water table decline becomes profound and the temporal drawdown curve exhibits a ﬂattened seg-
                                           ment in the second segment. In the third segment, the pumped water is from both the aquifer storage and the
                                           pore space on the water table.
                                           To examine the effect of gravity drainage from the pore space, Boulton (1954a) proposed an analytical model
                                           for CRP tests in an unconﬁned aquifer by embedding a convolution integral term in the ﬂow equation repre-
                                           senting the delayed gravity drainage as an exponential decay function of time. He developed the model based
                                           on the Dupuit‐Forchheimer assumption along with an empirical parameter named delay index in the con-
                                           volution integral term. Later, a variety of approaches incorporated the effect of gravity drainage on uncon-
                                           ﬁned ﬂow by either considering the unsaturated zone (e.g., Kroszynski & Dagan, 1975; Mathias & Butler,
                                           2006; Mishra & Neuman, 2010; Tartakovsky & Neuman, 2007) or embedding the effect in a free surface
                                           equation (e.g., Boulton, 1954b; Dagan, 1967; Malama, 2011; Moench, 1995; Neuman, 1972). Boulton
                                           (1954b) derived a linearized free surface equation in his CRP model considering radial and vertical ﬂows
                                           but neglecting aquifer storage. Dagan (1967) extended Boulton's (1954b) model to account for the effect of
                                           well partial penetration. Neuman (1972) further included aquifer storage in the model for a fully penetrating

             that the use of multiple convolution terms may be overparameterized. Recently, Malama (2011) proposed

             the nonlinear free surface equation. His model was applied to estimate the speciﬁc yield of the unconﬁned

             the models of Neuman (1974), Moench (1995), and Moench et al. (2001) with the WTAQ software package
             (Barlow & Moench, 1999). The results suggest that the speciﬁc yield is underestimated by Neuman's model
             as compared with laboratory outcomes.
             The capillary fringe above the water table is a tension‐saturated zone because the pores there are saturated
             with negative pressure (Freeze & Cherry, 1979). It may raise the water from 2.5 cm in ﬁne gravel to 200 cm in
             silt (Todd & Mays, 2005) and could have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the hydrologic process in shallow water
             table regimes (Gillham, 1984). When the water table declines due to pumping, the gravity may cause the
             pore water drainage from the capillary fringe to the aquifer. Under this circumstance, the gravity increas-
             ingly affects the drainage process in the late ﬁrst segment and the early second segment of the S‐shaped
             drawdown curve. Nwankwor et al. (1992) conﬁrmed that the capillary fringe plays a role in the drainage pro-
             cess within the ﬁrst segment of the unconﬁned temporal drawdown curve by conducting a pumping test at
             Canadian Forces Base Borden, Ontario, Canada.
             After the pore water drained above the water table, the curvature of the air‐water interface within the soil
             particles becomes sharper with increasing capillary suction. Large pores in the unsaturated zone may empty
             under lower suction, while small pores with the interfaces (menisci) of sharper curvature would empty at
             higher suction (Bear, 1979, p. 198). A certain amount of water in the pore is retained by the capillary force
             against the gravitational force. It takes time to complete the drainage, and the time lag should be taken into
             account (Bear, 1979, p. 206). Nwankwor et al. (1992) stated that when the rate of the water table declining is
             greater than the water content proﬁle moving rate, more water volume would be stored above the water
             table than that under the hydrostatic condition. It is called excess storage deﬁned in their work. Similarly,
             Bevan et al. (2005) conducted a 7‐day CRP test in an unconﬁned aquifer at Canadian Forces Base Borden,
             Ontario, Canada. They also found the same phenomenon (deﬁned as excess capillary fringe storage in their
             study) indicating that the pore water in the capillary fringe would not instantaneously ﬂow to the aquifer
             once the water table declines. The pore water from excess storage then gradually recharges to the aquifer
             below. Therefore, the capillary force leads to delayed gravity drainage over the entire second segment and
             the early third segment of the drawdown curve.
             Most existing analytical models scarcely considered the effect of the capillary fringe on the unconﬁned
             ﬂow due to its complexity in analysis. As mentioned above, some researchers proposed physically based
             models considering the unsaturated ﬂow (e.g., Kroszynski & Dagan, 1975; Liang et al., 2017; Mishra &
             Neuman, 2010; Tartakovsky & Neuman, 2007). They incorporated a saturated capillary fringe extending
             from the water table (i.e., zero pressure) to the elevation where the negative pressure head is equal to
             the air entry pressure. Although these models are comprehensive to depict the unconﬁned ﬂow for a
             CRP, more information on the unsaturated zone may be difﬁcult to obtain quantitatively. On the other
             hand, Neuman's (1972, 1974) models were commonly used in head predictions and parameter estimations
             for ﬁeld CRP problems. The value of the speciﬁc yield estimated by Neuman's model is usually

             3. Results and Discussion
             Consider a 30‐m thickness unconﬁned aquifer with Kr = 1 × 10−4 m/s, Kz = 5 × 10−5 m/s, Ss = 1 × 10−4 m−1,
             and Sy = 0.2. The pumping well is screened between z = 0 and z = 15 m (i.e., d = 0 and l = 15 m) with well
             radius rw and casing radius rc, both being 0.05 m. The pumping rate Q is 10−3 m3/s. The predicted drawdown
             is vertically averaged for a fully penetrating observation well located at 5 m from the pumping well. All the
             default values are used in the following analyses.

             4. Concluding Remarks
             This study proposes a new analytical model for describing the spatiotemporal drawdown distribution
             induced by a CRP test in an unconﬁned aquifer considering the lagging effect proposed by Lin and Yeh
             (2017) on the water table decline. Two lag times (i.e., τq and τs) are introduced to Darcy's law for developing
             a new linearized free surface equation describing the water table decline. The model incorporates the well-
             bore storage effect in the inner boundary condition describing the ﬂux across the wellbore. The drawdown
             solution is developed by the use of the Laplace transform and Weber transform. With the present solution,
             the sensitivity analysis is carried out to explore the inﬂuence of the changes in aquifer parameters on the
             temporal drawdown distributions. In addition, the present solution is applied to estimate the aquifer para-
             meters via nonlinear least squares method for the CRP tests performed in unconﬁned aquifers at the sites
             of Cape Cod, Massachusetts; Canadian Forces Base Borden, Ontario; and Saint Pardon de Conques,
             Gironde (France). Our new ﬁndings are given below:
             1. The present linearized free surface equation can reduce to that of Neuman (1972) when τs = 0 and τq = 0.
                It can also reduce to that of Moench (1995) when τqD = 0 and τsD = 1/α1D.
             2. A larger value of τsD produces a larger drawdown; it might be attributed to the fact that the effect of the
                capillary force slows down the drainage process. On the other hand, a larger τqD causes a smaller draw-
                down. The reason may be that more water is released from the pore spaces in the capillary fringe to the
                aquifer due to the increase of the vertical hydraulic drawdown.
             3. The sensitivity analysis indicates the drawdown is most sensitive to the change in Kr, followed by Kz, Sy,
                τs, τq, and Ss. The change in τs causes a more signiﬁcant effect on the drawdown than that in τq.
             4. The present solution provides very good ﬁts to ﬁeld observed drawdown data as compared with the exist-
                ing solutions. The values of Sy estimated by the present solution with the drawdown data obtained by
                CRP tests in the Cape Cod, Borden, and Saint Pardon de Conques sites fall in the reasonable range
                obtained from laboratory outcomes.
             5. The analysis of the drawdown data indicates each of the estimated lag times decreases linearly with
                increasing observed distance on the logarithmic scale for CRP tests at the Cape Cod, Borden, and Saint
                Pardon de Conques sites except for the case of τq for the Borden site. It is consistent with the ﬁndings
                of ﬁeld observations reported in Bevan et al.'s (2005) study which stated the capillary rise increases with
                decreasing distance to the pumping well.
